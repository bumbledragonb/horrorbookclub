Top Ten Horror Books of All Time

Books on this list were chosen by a poll of our readers.

*Disclaimer: We do not make any money from our links, we just aim to support independent booksellers.

1. The Haunting of Hill House

Shirley Jackson

Shirley Jackson is one of our favourite horror writers and has managed the incredible feat of being on the top ten list twice. We just cannot get enough of her writing and characters. The Haunting of Hill House is a horror classic that most of our readers are now familiar with, but can you believe she was out of print in the UK in the 2000s? We are so glad this has been rectified, and she has now taken her rightful place in the pantheon of horror authors. This book has everything you could hope for from a haunted house novel and one of the best opening paragraphs in all literature.

2. Dracula

Bram Stoker

Though vampires weren’t invented by Bram Stoker, this is the novel that has solidified our idea of the vampire mythology and, in our humble opinion, it has yet to be surpassed. Count Dracula has now taken many shapes in our popular culture – even comedic ones – but this does not take away the power and horror of the original. Pick up for the gothic atmosphere and stay for the exquisite writing. “Listen to them, the children of the night. What music they make!”

3. Frankenstein

Mary Shelley

The story goes that Frankenstein came to Mary Shelley in a dream, when she and her author friends were competing to come up with ghost stories. And what an enduring dream this has been. The creature is one of the best Romantic anti-heroes and Frankenstein is the archetype of the mad scientist. The beauty and horror of this book is that it will have you pitying and cheering the murderous creature on by the end.

4. Beauty

Aliya Whiteley

Aliya Whiteley’s short but brutal post-apocalyptic novella turned the genre on its head upon release in 2014. In creating a world where a fungal infection has killed off every woman, Whiteley manages to find the perfect setting to examine gender, and the impact gender roles have on society. A bold and imaginative vision, there is something telling about the way the surviving men all gather together in solidarity, only to fall apart the moment they find a glimmer of hope.

5. Cipher

Kathe Koja

It seems almost inexplicable that, until a new edition was released in 2020, The Cipher had been out of print almost since its publication in 1991. For almost thirty years this weird and twisted classic has been slipping under the radar. We hope that things are beginning to change. Nicholas and Nakota’s experiences after discovering a newly-formed black hole in a second storey storage room of their rundown apartment building make for irresistible reading. Taking the most unexpected route at every turn, The Cipher might be the most underrated classic of the horror genre.

6. The Bloody Chamber

Angela Carter

One of the greatest writers of the 20th century, Angela Carter influenced many of our favourite fantasy writers during her career. The short story collection The Bloody Chamber may be the peak of her work, though. Extracting ideas from well-known fairy tales, Carter offers up dark, twisted takes on familiar stories. In doing so, she both reverts the genre back to something echoing the tone of the Brothers Grimm whilst also setting up an entire fantasy/horror sub-genre that persists to this day.

7. Songs of a Dead Dreamer

Thomas Ligotti

Thomas Ligotti’s debut collection, Songs of a Dead Dreamer, felt like a new awakening for the genre upon its release in 1985. This is all the more surprising when you consider the obvious influences of Lovecraft and Poe upon the work held within. Songs of a Dead Dreamer is a reminder that horror is a fantastically varied genre, but also that it can forever be renewed, refreshed, and reimagined for new generations.

8. We Have Always Lived in The Castle

Shirley Jackson

The Queen of Horror returns with another creeping volume of dread and discomfort. We Have Always Lived in the Castle was the last book Shirley Jackson wrote before her death at just 48 years of age. Like Angela Carter, hers was a career cut short by illness, but long remembered thanks to the incredible legacy of her written work. Few authors have ever left such a powerful final novel as Jackson’s.

9. Beloved

Toni Morrison

Horror is often merely a cover for the exploration of complex literary themes, and Toni Morrison’s Beloved is amongst the best examples of this. Telling the story of a former slave who believes she is haunted by the ghost of her eldest daughter, who died at the age of two, Beloved looks at the psychological impact of slavery, and the nature of mother/daughter relationships amongst other ideas. It is horror at its finest - deeply troubling, deeply moving, and vitally important.

10. Broken Monsters

Lauren Beukes

Lauren Beukes’ fourth novel, Broken Monsters, is a million miles from Morrison’s Beloved, both in theme and plot. But it shares one important quality - the exploration of our troubled world through the medium of the horror genre. From the twisted half boy/half deer corpse Detective Versado is investigating to the paedophiles her daughter baits online, the world of Broken Monsters holds a black mirror up to our society and - perhaps most horrifyingly of all - dares to make a murderer the most sympathetic character of the lot.
